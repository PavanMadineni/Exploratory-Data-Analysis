In [8]:
Age = data$Age
Weight = data$Weight
Height = data$Height

Age.cat = rep(NA, nrow(data))
Age.cat[Age <= quantile(Age, 1/3)] = "Young"
Age.cat[Age > quantile(Age, 1/3) & Age <= quantile(Age, 2/3)] = "Medium Age"
Age.cat[Age > quantile(Age, 2/3)] = "Old"
Age.cat = factor(Age.cat, levels = c("Young", "Medium Age", "Old Age"))

Weight.cat = rep(NA, nrow(data))
Weight.cat[Weight <= quantile(Weight, 1/3)] = "Light"
Weight.cat[Weight > quantile(Weight, 1/3) & Weight <= quantile(Weight, 2/3)] = "Medium Weight"
Weight.cat[Weight > quantile(Weight, 2/3)] = "Heavy"
Weight.cat = factor(Weight.cat, levels = c("Light", "Medium Weight", "Heavy"))

Height.cat = rep(NA, nrow(data))
Height.cat[Height <= quantile(Height, 1/3)] = "Short"
Height.cat[Height > quantile(Height, 1/3) & Height <= quantile(Height, 2/3)] = "Medium Height"
Height.cat[Height > quantile(Height, 2/3)] = "Tall"
Height.cat = factor(Height.cat, levels = c("Short", "Medium Height", "Tall"))

data.cat = data.frame(data, Age.cat, Weight.cat, Height.cat)

It can be clearly observed that there aren't any awkwards trends prevalant in the data.

As you go move from top to bottom i.e with increasing weights, the avg blood pressure increases for both male and female.

Also as you move from left to right, i.e. with increasing heights, the avg blood pressure appears to reduce a bit.

It can be seen from the graph that both men and women who are short and heavy seem to have the highest average systolic blood pressure which sounds very practical too.

In general, in every zone i.e. for a particular height, weight and age, men seem to have greater bllod pressure when compared with women.

It can observed that People who are heavy are more prone to being diabetic in general.

The chance of being diabetic clearly has an increasing trend with age.

Among the people who are heavy, both male and female, the shorter ones have a stronger tendency to being diabetic than the taller people which also sounds practically plausible.

With the increasing weight the chance of being diabetic clearly increases but it has a slightly downward trend with both weight and Height simultaneously increasing.

Also in order to enhance the visual appeal of the graphs, it appears wise to factor on weights than on heights since weights appear to have a stronger influence on the chance of being diabetic than Height.

It can be clearly observed that the sleep pattern does have a significant impact on people affected with Diabetes.

People with healthy sleep tend to be less prone to diabetes, the curve depicting the chance of diabetes does not curve upward.

There is a surprisingly less chance of being diabetic for people of low weight and unhealthy sleep patterns. This could be because of not having enough data points to describe the trend in this region.

The reason for considering weight as an explanatory variable over height is becuase being tall or short does not inherently cause any health disorders but excess or underweight can definitely lead to ill-health.

Since Gender doesnt really seem to have an impact, I considered to opt it out of the analysis here.

It can be seen that Unhealthy sleep patterns, be it lack of sleep or excess sleep increases the risk of being diabetic even if you fall under a healthy weight.

Lack of sleep in particular has a steep rise towards the chance of being diabetic for all the weights and heights combinations.

As explained in above graphs also, weight plays a more significant role than height in determining the risk of being diabetic. That is probably the reason inspite of healthy sleep, excess weight can be more prone towards being diabetic.

To sum up, the chance of being diabetic for a person increases with age, weight and unhealthy sleep patterns, be it lack of sleep or excess sleep.
